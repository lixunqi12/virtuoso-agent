Recent work on LLM-driven analog design splits, by what the LLM is
asked to do, into four clusters. We organize the literature along
those clusters, identify the closest comparison points, and present
Table 1 mapping our four contributions onto the gap each closes.

**A. LLM-as-designer (netlist generation from spec).**
AnalogCoder [Lai et al., AAAI 2025] frames analog design as a
training-free code-generation problem; AnalogCoder-Pro [2025]
extends it with a Qwen-LoRA variant. This cluster is
**complementary** to our work: AnalogCoder operates on open-PDK
abstractions, while we assume a foundry-bound schematic exists
(`--lib --cell` is the agent's entry point) and optimize *against*
it. A designer who used AnalogCoder to seed a topology could then
close the loop with Safe-Bridge to harden it for production silicon.

**B. LLM-as-optimizer / agentic sizing (the closest cluster).**
ADO-LLM [Yin et al., ICCAD 2024] couples Bayesian optimization with
an LLM that proposes high-quality in-context demonstrations.
EEsizer [Liu et al., NEWCAS 2025] runs a ReAct-style closed-loop
agent over Ngspice with no external optimizer and benchmarks across
eight commercial LLMs. AnaFlow [Ahmadzadeh & Gielen, ICCAD 2025] is
the closest direct methodological competitor — a four-agent workflow
(circuit-explainer, matching-finder, DC-goal-setter, sizer) with
explainable reasoning and sample-efficient sizing on BSIM 45 nm
predictive models. LEDRO [Kochar et al., ICLAD 2025] uses the LLM
not to propose points but to shrink TuRBO's search box, orthogonal
to our agentic spec-loop. Two concurrent 2025–2026 papers extend the
SOTA: AutoSizer [Cheng et al., arXiv 2602.02849, 2026] benchmarks
reflective multi-agent meta-optimization across 24 circuits;
AnalogSAGE [Wang et al., arXiv 2512.22435, 2025] adds stratified
memory and experience replay. None of B's papers validate on a
disclosed industrial-node PDK; none reports a failure-mode taxonomy
or per-iteration token cost as primary results; **none evaluates a
domestic-China LLM**. Our SP1–SP4 target exactly this gap.

**AnalogAgent contests SP1 directly.** AnalogAgent [Bao et al.,
arXiv 2603.23910, 2026] is the only paper that explicitly targets
NDA-constrained deployment. They solve it by restricting inference
to tiny on-premise Qwen3 1.7B-14B open-weight models running fully
offline. Our Safe-Bridge filter takes the *complementary* approach:
admit any cloud LLM (including state-of-the-art reasoning models),
prevent PDK content from ever crossing the trust boundary. The two
stories bracket the industrial-deployment design space.

**C. Benchmarks.**
AnalogGym [Li et al., ICCAD 2024] is the de-facto open benchmark
suite for analog-ML work — 30 topologies across 5 categories,
Spectre / HSPICE / Ngspice support, sky130 as the disclosed open
path. Safe-Bridge's spec evaluator borrows the YAML-metric idiom
from this lineage (`signals:` / `windows:` / `metrics:`, see
`src/spec_evaluator.py`) but binds it to a closed, industrial PDK;
the metrics themselves are written by the user against their own
testbench. AnalogGym contributes the *measurement language*; we
close the *trust boundary* that lets you use it on NDA-bound silicon.

**D. Adjacent EDA agent work (digital / RTL).**
ChatEDA [He et al., 2023] and ChipNeMo [NVIDIA, 2024] frame RTL-side
EDA tasks as LLM agentic workflows. We adopt the repair-prompt idiom
in `src/agent.py` (one-shot contract-violation retry) from this
lineage and cite for design-pattern context, not as a direct baseline.

**Differentiation.** Table 1 summarizes how the closest comparison
points score against SP1–SP4. Per the verification scan, only SP4
(domestic-China LLM eval) and SP1 NDA-defense remain fully ✗ across
the competitor set; SP3 per-iter cost and SP4 open-LLM eval flip to
*partial* for several systems where token counts or non-commercial
LLM coverage is reported in passing but is not the primary
contribution. AnalogAgent gets its own row to capture the
*orthogonal-but-real* SP1 NDA defense story.
